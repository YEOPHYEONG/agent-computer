# Claim Verification Map

## Status Legend

- Verified: usable with citation and caveat.
- Use With Caveat: usable only with explicit limits.
- Needs Verification: do not use as a firm claim yet.
- Avoid: too weak, too broad, or too investment-like.

| Claim | Status | Evidence | Required Caveat |
|---|---|---|---|
| U.S. data centers used 176 TWh of electricity in 2023, 4.4% of U.S. electricity consumption. | Verified | DOE/LBNL 2024 report. | Total data centers, not AI-only. |
| U.S. data centers could use 325-580 TWh in 2028, equal to 6.7%-12.0% of U.S. electricity consumption. | Verified | DOE/LBNL 2024 report. | Scenario range, not a point forecast. |
| Data centers are a significant driver of near-term U.S. electricity demand growth. | Verified | EIA January 2026 STEO release; DOE resources page; LBNL report. | Data centers are one driver among manufacturing, electrification, industrial load, and climate/weather effects. |
| Electricity is a global absolute scarcity problem for AI. | Avoid | National/global shares remain modest relative to total power systems. | Better claim: local, timely, firm, deliverable power is becoming a bottleneck. |
| AI data centers are the sole cause of U.S. power price increases. | Avoid | Price changes have multiple causes: fuel, weather, retirements, congestion, capacity market design, load growth. | Discuss data centers as a contributing driver where source-backed. |
| PJM and FERC show data-center load is now a market-design and cost-allocation issue. | Verified | FERC co-location action; FERC State of the Markets; PJM forecast materials. | PJM is a key case, not automatically nationwide. |
| AEP Ohio's data-center queue shows announced/requested load can be much larger than contracted load. | Verified | AEP Ohio 2026 update. | Company claim and one service territory; use as an example of attrition/filtering. |
| Hyperscaler capex has made energy and data-center power a strategic corporate issue. | Verified | Microsoft annual report; Alphabet 10-K; Meta 10-K; Amazon filings/official releases. | Capex is not all electricity; energy dependence is one part of broader infrastructure. |
| Cooling and water are bottlenecks for AI data centers. | Use With Caveat | LBNL report; company sustainability disclosures. | Often site-specific; cooling technology can trade water, power, capex, and density. |
| Renewable procurement solves the AI electricity bottleneck. | Avoid | Hyperscaler disclosures show procurement scale; IEA/DOE note grids and storage still matter. | Annual matching is not the same as local hourly power supply. |
| Nuclear will solve the 2026-2030 AI power bottleneck. | Avoid | Constellation/Microsoft restart is relevant; SMR timelines mostly extend beyond 2030. | Existing nuclear and restarts are nearer term; SMRs are uncertain optionality. |
| Gas is likely to be an important near-term bridge for firm AI/data-center power in the U.S. | Use With Caveat | IEA supply mix; EIA/DOE/NERC reliability context; company power-developer materials. | Emissions, fuel supply, turbine lead times, permitting, and policy are constraints. |
| Electrical equipment and grid infrastructure are closer to the bottleneck than many AI-branded equities. | Use With Caveat | NERC/FERC/DOE/LBNL/IEA source cluster; market map. | Profit capture depends on backlog, margins, supply chain, execution, and valuation. |
| Electricity bottlenecks imply specific buy/sell recommendations. | Avoid | Outside educational scope. | No recommendations, targets, or model portfolio. |

## Claims To Ask Evidence-Verifier To Stress-Test

- Exact PJM data-center load growth through 2030.
- FERC 50 GW end-2025 data-center capacity definition.
- NERC 2025 large-load reliability language and any specific outage/stability incident.
- Meta/Amazon 2025/2026 capex numbers from primary sources.
- Transformer and switchgear lead-time/supply-deficit claims from primary or near-primary sources.
